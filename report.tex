\section*{Problem 1: MNL Model}
\label{sec:p1}

Each search query \texttt{srch\_id} is a choice set $S$ of displayed hotels.
Hotel $j$ has utility
\[
u_j \;=\; \beta_0 + \sum_{i=1}^{8} \beta_i\, x_{ji},
\qquad
v_j \;=\; e^{u_j},
\qquad
\mathbb{P}(j \mid S) \;=\; \frac{v_j}{1 + \sum_{p \in S} v_p},
\]
with the outside option (no booking) carrying weight $1$.
The eight features are star rating, review score, hotel brand, location score,
accessibility score, log historical price, displayed price, and promotion flag.

\paragraph{Estimation.}
We maximise the query-level log-likelihood
\[
\mathcal{L}(\beta) \;=\;
\sum_{q \in Q}\!\Big[\, \mathbf{1}_{\{y_q \ne 0\}}\, u_{q,y_q}
  - \log\!\Big(1 + \sum_{p \in S_q} e^{u_{q,p}}\Big) \Big],
\]
which is concave in $\beta$, by a damped Newton method with backtracking
(see \texttt{scripts/mnl\_utils.py}).
Continuous features are $z$-scored before estimation; binary features stay
in $\{0,1\}$. The reported coefficients are then transformed back to the
\emph{raw} (un-standardised) scale so that the small datasets can be scored
directly without any rescaling step.

\paragraph{Estimated coefficients (raw scale).}
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lr}

Coefficient & Estimate \\
\midrule

star rating                         & $+0.4761$ \\
review score                        & $+0.1199$ \\
brand indicator                     & $+0.2299$ \\
location score                      & $+0.0163$ \\

\paragraph{Interpretation of every coefficient.}
A coefficient $\beta_i$ measures how much one extra unit of feature $x_i$
shifts the log-odds of choosing a hotel relative to the outside option.
For binary features the size of the shift equals the coefficient itself
(the feature flips by exactly one unit); for continuous features the
shift is proportional to the unit of the feature.
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Intercept $\beta_0 = -2.8153$ (large negative).}
    The baseline utility of a generic hotel is well below the outside
    option's utility of $0$. Combined with the reasonable choice
    probabilities we get on training queries, this says that customers
    do \emph{not} book by default --- they need positive evidence
    (good reviews, brand, promotion, etc.) to push utility above the
    outside option. A near-zero intercept would have implied the average
    hotel is already as attractive as not booking, which is not what we
    see in the data ($\sim\!30\%$ of queries end without a booking).
  \item \textbf{Star rating $+0.4761$ (large positive).}
    A one-star jump (e.g.\ from $3\!\to\!4$) raises utility by about $0.48$,
    multiplying $v_j$ by $e^{0.48}\approx 1.6$. A coefficient close to
    zero would mean star rating does not drive choice; the value here
    instead tells us star rating is one of the strongest signals.
  \item \textbf{Review score $+0.1199$ (small positive).}
    Each unit of review score (on a $0$--$5$ scale) raises utility by
    only $\sim\!0.12$, which is modest. Once star rating is in the model,
    review score adds little extra information --- the two are
    correlated, and the joint estimation pushes most of the quality
    signal onto star rating. This is a typical co-linear-feature
    pattern, not evidence that reviews are unimportant in isolation.
  \item \textbf{Brand indicator $+0.2299$ (medium positive).}
    Branded hotels enjoy roughly a $0.23$ utility boost over independents,
    multiplying $v_j$ by $e^{0.23}\approx 1.26$. Brand carries a real
    booking premium, but smaller than star rating or promotion.
  \item \textbf{Location score $+0.0163$ (near zero).}
    Once star rating, brand, and accessibility are accounted for,
    Expedia's location score barely moves the choice. The near-zero
    coefficient indicates location is largely already proxied by the
    other features and adds little marginal signal.
  \item \textbf{Accessibility score $+0.5629$ (largest single positive).}
    This is the strongest non-intercept positive coefficient. The
    accessibility score has very small variance ($\sigma \approx 0.077$),
    so a one-unit raw change is rare; on the standardised scale this
    feature still has the second-largest effect. It captures something
    about ease of access (transport, parking, etc.) that customers value
    strongly.
  \item \textbf{Log historical price $-0.0374$ (small negative).}
    Slightly negative once displayed price and quality features are in
    the model. The historical price proxy is highly correlated with
    other quality signals, so its marginal effect after controlling for
    them is small and absorbs a tiny negative residual. This is a
    joint-estimation artefact rather than evidence that pricier-history
    hotels are disliked.
  \item \textbf{Displayed price $-0.00732$ (negative; large in dollar
    terms).}
    Per-dollar, the coefficient looks tiny, but prices typically span
    \$50--\$500, so a \$100 price increase reduces utility by
    $0.732$ ($v_j$ scaled by $e^{-0.732}\approx 0.48$, i.e.\ roughly
    halved). In economic terms this is a large effect and is
    \emph{the} essential ingredient for Problem~3 to have a finite
    interior optimum --- a positive or near-zero $\beta_{\text{price}}$
    would mean revenue increases without bound as we raise prices.
  \item \textbf{Promotion flag $+0.4540$ (large positive).}
    Promoted listings see their preference weight multiplied by
    $e^{0.45}\approx 1.57$. This is essentially the same magnitude as
    moving up one star and is the largest binary effect in the model:
    customers click and book promoted hotels disproportionately, even
    after price is controlled for.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Summary of magnitudes.}
The strongest positive drivers of choice are accessibility, star rating,
and promotion; the dominant negative driver is displayed price. Review
score, brand, location, and historical price contribute smaller marginal
signals, often because they are partially absorbed by correlated
features. The negative intercept means the outside option is the
default, which is consistent with the empirical query-level booking
rate of $\sim 70\%$.

\paragraph{Convergence.}
Final gradient norm is at machine precision, so the (concave) MLE has
converged to its global maximum. The uniform-choice baseline log-likelihood
is $-22\,856.21$; the fitted model improves on that baseline by $2\,244.88$
log-likelihood units, confirming that the fit is meaningful.

\section*{Problem 2: Assortment Optimization under MNL}
\label{sec:p2}

Given the hotels in \texttt{dataX.csv}, choose a subset $S$ to display so as
to maximise the expected revenue
\[
R(S) \;=\; \frac{\sum_{j \in S} p_j v_j}{1 + \sum_{j \in S} v_j},
\qquad
v_j \;=\; \exp(\hat u_j),
\]
where $\hat u_j$ uses the Problem~1 raw-scale coefficients.

\paragraph{Solution method.}
Under unconstrained MNL assortment optimization, the optimum is
\emph{revenue-ordered} (Talluri and van~Ryzin, 2004): there exists a price
threshold $p^\star$ such that $S^* = \{j : p_j \ge p^\star\}$.
Sorting hotels by displayed price in decreasing order and evaluating the
revenue of every prefix therefore yields the exact optimum in
$O(n \log n)$. The implementation is in
\texttt{scripts/problem2\_assortment.py}.

\paragraph{Results.}
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lrrl}
\toprule
Dataset & $|S^*|$ & $R(S^*)$ & $S^*$ (1-based indices) \\
\midrule
\texttt{data1.csv} & 18 & $107.35$ & $\{1,2,3,4,5,6,7,13,16,18,19,20,21,22,23,24,25,27\}$ \\
\texttt{data2.csv} & 10 & $131.11$ & $\{1,2,7,8,9,10,11,22,24,26\}$ \\
\texttt{data3.csv} & 18 & $121.05$ & $\{1,2,3,4,5,6,8,9,11,12,14,15,16,17,19,20,24,25\}$ \\
\texttt{data4.csv} & 11 & $97.41$  & $\{4,5,7,9,11,16,19,20,21,22,27\}$ \\
\bottomrule

\end{center}

\paragraph{Local check.}
For every reported assortment we verified that no single-item add or drop
improves revenue, consistent with the revenue-ordered theorem.

\section*{Problem 3: Pricing under MNL}
\label{sec:p3}

Display all $n$ hotels in \texttt{dataX.csv} and choose new prices
$p_1,\dots,p_n$ to maximise
\[
R(p) \;=\; \frac{\sum_j p_j\, v_j(p_j)}{1 + \sum_j v_j(p_j)},
\qquad
v_j(p_j) \;=\; \exp\!\big(a_j + b\, p_j\big),
\]
where $a_j$ is the Problem-1 utility stripped of the price term and
$b = \hat\beta_{\text{price}} = -0.00732 < 0$.

\paragraph{First-order condition: full derivation.}
Introduce shorthand for the quantities that appear repeatedly:
\[
V(p) \;=\; \sum_{k=1}^{n} v_k(p_k),
\qquad
D(p) \;=\; 1 + V(p),
\qquad
N(p) \;=\; \sum_{k=1}^{n} p_k\, v_k(p_k),
\]
so that $R(p) = N(p) / D(p)$.

\textit{Step 1: derivative of a single weight.}
Because $v_k = \exp(a_k + b\, p_k)$ depends only on its own price,
\[
\frac{\partial v_k}{\partial p_j}
  \;=\;
  \begin{cases}
    b\, v_j & \text{if } k = j, \\
    0 & \text{otherwise}.
  \end{cases}
\]

\textit{Step 2: derivative of $V$ and $D$.}
Summing over $k$ kills every term except $k=j$,
\[
\frac{\partial V}{\partial p_j} \;=\; b\, v_j,
\qquad
\frac{\partial D}{\partial p_j} \;=\; \frac{\partial V}{\partial p_j} \;=\; b\, v_j.
\]

\textit{Step 3: derivative of the numerator $N$.}
Only the $k=j$ term in $N = \sum_k p_k v_k$ depends on $p_j$ (both through
the factor $p_k$ and through $v_k$). Apply the product rule to that term:
\[
\frac{\partial N}{\partial p_j}
  \;=\;
  \frac{\partial}{\partial p_j}\big(p_j v_j\big)
  \;=\;
  v_j \;+\; p_j \cdot b\, v_j
  \;=\;
  v_j\,(1 + b\, p_j).
\]

\textit{Step 4: quotient rule for $R = N/D$.}
\[
\frac{\partial R}{\partial p_j}
  \;=\;
  \frac{\dfrac{\partial N}{\partial p_j}\, D \;-\; N\, \dfrac{\partial D}{\partial p_j}}{D^{\,2}}
  \;=\;
  \frac{v_j(1+b\,p_j)\, D \;-\; N\, b\, v_j}{D^{\,2}}.
\]
Factor $v_j$ out of the numerator:
\[
\frac{\partial R}{\partial p_j}
  \;=\;
  \frac{v_j\,\big[\, (1 + b\, p_j)\, D \;-\; b\, N \,\big]}{D^{\,2}}.
\]

\textit{Step 5: substitute $N = R\, D$.}
Since $R = N/D$ implies $N = R D$, we can replace $N$ in the bracket:
\[
(1 + b\, p_j)\, D \;-\; b\, N
  \;=\;
  (1 + b\, p_j)\, D \;-\; b\, R\, D
  \;=\;
  D\,\big[\, 1 + b\, p_j - b\, R \,\big].
\]
Therefore
\[
\frac{\partial R}{\partial p_j}
  \;=\;
  \frac{v_j\, D \,\big[\, 1 + b\, p_j - b\, R \,\big]}{D^{\,2}}
  \;=\;
  \frac{v_j\,\big[\, 1 + b\, (p_j - R) \,\big]}{D}.
\]

\textit{Step 6: setting the derivative to zero.}
At an interior optimum we need $\partial R/\partial p_j = 0$ for every $j$.
Since $v_j > 0$ and $D > 0$, the only way this can hold is
\[
1 + b\,(p_j - R) \;=\; 0
\qquad\Longleftrightarrow\qquad
p_j \;=\; R - \tfrac{1}{b}.
\]
With $b < 0$, $-1/b = 1/|b| > 0$, so we obtain the clean form
\[
\boxed{\;p_j^\star \;=\; R^\star + \tfrac{1}{|b|}.\;}
\]

\textit{Step 7: consequence.}
The right-hand side does not depend on $j$. \emph{Every optimal price is
identical.} Writing the common optimal price $p^\star$ and the resulting
revenue $R^\star = R(p^\star,\dots,p^\star)$, the pair satisfies the
implicit fixed-point system
\[
p^\star = R^\star + \tfrac{1}{|b|},
\qquad
R^\star = p^\star \cdot
  \frac{\sum_j e^{a_j + b\, p^\star}}{1 + \sum_j e^{a_j + b\, p^\star}}.
\]
This system has a unique solution (it can be reduced to a transcendental
equation of Lambert-$W$ type). Because the optimum is a single scalar,
we solve it directly by golden-section search over $p$, which is exact
under the model and not a relaxation
(\texttt{scripts/problem3\_pricing.py}).

\paragraph{Results.}
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lrrr}
\toprule
Dataset & $p^\star$ (USD) & Current $R$ & Optimal $R^\star$ \\
\midrule
\texttt{data1.csv} & $314.36$ & $104.65$ & $177.81$ \\
\texttt{data2.csv} & $385.36$ & $113.36$ & $248.80$ \\
\texttt{data3.csv} & $313.03$ & $118.17$ & $176.47$ \\
\texttt{data4.csv} & $351.03$ & $\phantom{0}78.69$  & $214.47$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\paragraph{Sanity check.}
With $1/|b| = 136.56$, the identity $p^\star - R^\star = 1/|b|$ should hold
on every dataset. For \texttt{data1.csv}: $314.36-177.81=136.55$;
\texttt{data2.csv}: $385.36-248.80=136.56$; \texttt{data3.csv}:
$313.03-176.47=136.56$; \texttt{data4.csv}: $351.03-214.47=136.56$.
All four match $136.56$ to two decimals, confirming the solution.

\paragraph{Why a single common price.}
Identical optimal prices are a direct consequence of using \emph{one shared
linear price coefficient} in Problem~1. Differentiated optimal prices would
require a hotel-specific or interacted price sensitivity, which the
assignment does not ask for. Under the fitted model the reported solution
is exact.

\section*{Problem 4: Mixture of MNL}
\label{sec:p4}

We define two customer types from the booking window:
\[
\text{type 1 (early)} \iff \text{bw} \ge 7,
\qquad
\text{type 2 (late)}  \iff \text{bw} < 7,
\]
following the assignment instruction that "if the booking window is less
than 7 days, we define it as a late booking and early otherwise."
For each type $k \in \{1,2\}$ the per-type weights are
\[
v_{j,k} \;=\; \exp\!\Big(\beta_{0k} + \sum_{i=1}^{8} \beta_{ik}\, x_{ji}\Big),
\]
and the mixture choice probability is
\[
\mathbb{P}(j \mid S) \;=\; \sum_{k=1}^{2} \theta_k\,
  \frac{v_{j,k}}{1 + \sum_{p \in S} v_{p,k}}.
\]

\paragraph{Estimating $\theta_1, \theta_2$.}
Because the type is observable, we estimate $\theta_k$ as the share of
\emph{customers} (queries) of each type, not the share of rows:
\[
\hat\theta_1 \;=\; \frac{\#\{\text{queries with bw} \ge 7\}}{\#\text{queries}},
\qquad
\hat\theta_2 \;=\; 1 - \hat\theta_1.
\]

\paragraph{Per-type MLE.}
We then fit two MNLs with the same Newton procedure as Problem~1, but
restricted to the queries of each type. To make the resulting coefficients
directly comparable across the two types we use \emph{shared} feature scaling
(common means and standard deviations).

Both per-type log-likelihoods converged (gradient norms
$3.2\!\times\!10^{-9}$ and $1.2\!\times\!10^{-9}$).

\paragraph{Interpretation of every coefficient.}
We compare the early-booker and late-booker estimates feature by feature.
Differences below the $10^{-2}$ scale are essentially noise; the
substantive movements are in price, promotion, accessibility, location,
and the intercept.
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Intercept: early $-2.97$, late $-2.71$.}
    Both are large negatives, so the outside option is the default for
    both segments. The late intercept is somewhat less negative
    ($+0.27$ relative), meaning that holding all features at zero a
    late booker is closer to the booking margin than an early booker.
    This is consistent with the empirical fact that late bookers have
    a higher booking rate per query in the data.
  \item \textbf{Star rating: early $+0.44$, late $+0.54$.}
    Both segments value star rating, but late bookers value it
    \emph{more}. A late booker bumping a hotel from 3 to 4 stars sees
    $v_j$ multiplied by $e^{0.54}\approx 1.72$, vs.\ $1.55$ for an
    early booker. Possible interpretation: under time pressure, late
    bookers fall back on visible quality signals.
  \item \textbf{Review score: early $+0.14$, late $+0.11$.}
    Both small positives. Early bookers weigh review score slightly
    more, consistent with having time to read reviews; late bookers
    rely a touch more on the cruder star rating signal. The gap is
    small ($\sim\!0.03$), so this is a mild effect.
  \item \textbf{Brand: early $+0.21$, late $+0.26$.}
    Late bookers place a slightly larger premium on branded hotels
    ($e^{0.26}\approx 1.29$ vs.\ $1.23$). Brand functions as a
    risk-reducing heuristic when search time is short.
  \item \textbf{Location score: early $-0.016$, late $+0.065$.}
    A meaningful sign flip: early bookers' coefficient is essentially
    zero (or weakly negative once correlated features are partialled
    out), while late bookers reward better-located hotels modestly.
    Late bookers presumably care about getting straight to the
    destination, while early planners can substitute location for
    other amenities.
  \item \textbf{Accessibility: early $+0.75$, late $+0.33$.}
    The largest difference between the two segments. Early bookers
    weight accessibility more than \emph{twice} as heavily as late
    bookers. Travelers who plan ahead seem to prefer
    transit-/parking-accessible hotels, while last-minute bookers
    accept a worse access score.
  \item \textbf{Log historical price: early $-0.053$, late $-0.018$.}
    Both small and negative. Early bookers penalise historically
    expensive hotels slightly more. As in Problem~1, this feature is
    largely absorbed by displayed price and quality features, so the
    effect is residual.
  \item \textbf{Displayed price: early $-0.00590$, late $-0.00934$.}
    Late bookers are about $1.6\times$ more price sensitive. A
    \$100 price increase costs an early booker $0.59$ utility points
    ($v_j$ scaled by $e^{-0.59}\approx 0.55$); the same increase costs
    a late booker $0.93$ points ($v_j$ scaled by $0.39$). This is the
    most policy-relevant difference between the two segments and
    drives the divergence in optimal pricing.
  \item \textbf{Promotion: early $+0.38$, late $+0.55$.}
    Late bookers respond markedly more to promotions. Combined with
    higher price sensitivity, this paints a clear picture: late bookers
    are deal-seekers. The promotion effect for them is comparable to
    a one-star upgrade.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Summary.}
The biggest segment-level differences are: late bookers are more price
sensitive and more promo-responsive (a deal-seeking profile), while early
bookers care more about accessibility and slightly more about review
content (a planning profile). All other coefficient differences are
small and qualitatively consistent with the same overall ranking of
attributes.

\section*{Problem 5: Early vs.\ Late Reservations}
\label{sec:p5}

For each small dataset we compute three assortments:
\[
S \;=\; \arg\max_S \big[\theta_1 R_1(S) + \theta_2 R_2(S)\big],
\qquad
S_1 \;=\; \arg\max_S R_1(S),
\qquad
S_2 \;=\; \arg\max_S R_2(S),
\]
where $R_k(S) = \big(\sum_{j \in S} p_j v_{j,k}\big) / \big(1 + \sum_{j \in S} v_{j,k}\big)$.

\paragraph{Type-aware sub-problems $S_1, S_2$.}
Each is a single-class MNL assortment problem, so the revenue-ordered
theorem from Problem~2 applies directly.

\paragraph{Unknown-type problem $S$: integer program.}
The mixture objective is a sum of two fractional functions in the
binary selection vector, hence not revenue-ordered in general. The
assignment explicitly says "you need to solve an integer program here to
compute $S$." Our exact MILP formulation is below.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Decision variables}
\begin{tabular}{ll}
$x_j \in \{0,1\}$            & 1 if hotel $j$ is included in $S$, \\
$t_k \ge 0$, $k \in \{1,2\}$ & auxiliary variable equal to $1/(1+\sum_j v_{j,k} x_j)$, \\
$s_{j,k} \ge 0$              & McCormick linearisation of $t_k \cdot x_j$.
\end{tabular}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Objective}
\[
\max\;\; \sum_{j=1}^{n}\!\Big[
   \theta_1\, p_j\, v_{j,1}\, s_{j,1} +
   \theta_2\, p_j\, v_{j,2}\, s_{j,2}
\Big].
\]

\noindent\textbf{Normalisation constraints} (tie $t_k$ to $x$):
\[
t_k + \sum_{j=1}^{n} v_{j,k}\, s_{j,k} \;=\; 1, \qquad k \in \{1,2\}.
\]

\noindent\textbf{McCormick envelopes} for $s_{j,k} = t_k\cdot x_j$ with
$x_j \in \{0,1\}$ and $t_k \in [0,1]$:
\[
s_{j,k} \le t_k, \qquad
s_{j,k} \le x_j, \qquad
s_{j,k} \ge t_k + x_j - 1, \qquad
s_{j,k} \ge 0.
\]
Because $x_j$ is binary, the envelope is tight: at every integer feasible
solution we have $s_{j,k} = t_k\, x_j$ exactly, and the objective collapses
to the original mixture revenue. The MILP is therefore an \emph{exact
reformulation}, not a relaxation.

\paragraph{Solver.}
The MILP is solved with Gurobi (\texttt{gurobi\_cl}) on an LP file produced
by \texttt{scripts/problem5\_assortment.py}. A branch-and-bound fallback in
the same script solves the same problem exactly without Gurobi and is used
to cross-validate the MILP solution.

\section*{Problem 7: AI Agents as Customers}
\label{sec:p7}

\paragraph{Approach.}
We take \texttt{data.csv} and remove the booking column, then for each
search query we present the hotel attributes and customer context to an
AI agent and ask it to make a single choice: either one displayed hotel
(by \texttt{alt\_id}) or \texttt{NO\_PURCHASE}. The AI-generated choices
replace the original booking column, and we re-estimate the MNL of
Problem~1 on the AI-labelled dataset (7a, 7b). We then use the AI agent
as a held-out predictor: it is given a small set of labelled context
examples and asked to predict booking decisions on held-out real
queries, and its accuracy is compared against the MNL estimated in
Problem~1 and against a trivial baseline (7c).

\paragraph{AI agent and prompt.}
We use ChatGPT (GPT-5.5 Thinking). The exact prompt template for each
sampled query is documented in
\texttt{problem7/inputs/ai\_generation\_prompt\_template.txt} and asks
the AI to: (i) consume the customer/search context (booking window,
adults, children, rooms, Saturday-night flag), (ii) consume the table
of all hotel alternatives with their eight attributes, and (iii) return
exactly one choice (an \texttt{alt\_id} or \texttt{NO\_PURCHASE}).
Because in-chat prompting of all $8\,354$ queries is not feasible, the
prompt-response loop is performed manually on a random subsample.

\paragraph{7a --- AI-labelled booking dataset.}
We sample $500$ search queries (\texttt{numpy} random seed 42), retain
all alternatives in each sampled choice set ($9\,005$ hotel rows total),
and prompt the AI once per query. The AI selected a hotel in $402$
queries and chose \texttt{NO\_PURCHASE} in $98$. The AI's text answer
is then converted into a binary \texttt{booking\_ai} column on the same
row-per-hotel layout as \texttt{data.csv}: the chosen \texttt{alt\_id}
gets $1$, every other alternative in the same query gets $0$, and
\texttt{NO\_PURCHASE} queries get all-zero rows.

\paragraph{7b --- Re-estimated MNL on AI-generated bookings.}
We fit the MNL of Problem~1 on the $500$-query AI sample using the
\texttt{booking\_ai} column. Because the AI choices are highly
deterministic, the unregularised MLE is unstable; we therefore
minimise a ridge-penalised negative log-likelihood
\[
\min_\beta\; -\mathcal{L}_{\text{AI}}(\beta) \;+\; \lambda \sum_{i \ge 1} \beta_i^2,
\qquad \lambda = 1.
\]
$\lambda = 1$ is a stabilisation device, not a tuned hyper-parameter.
On the $500$-query fit the optimiser converges to a penalised NLL of
$269.05$ (unpenalised NLL $182.29$) in $17$ L-BFGS-B iterations.

The estimated AI coefficients (z-scored features within the AI sample)
are placed next to the Problem~1 normalised coefficients
(continuous z-scored on the full $153\,009$ rows, binaries unscaled):

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lrr}
\toprule
Coefficient & AI (ridge, $\lambda=1$) & Human (P1 normalised) \\
\midrule
intercept                    & $-3.4938$ & $-1.9815$ \\
prop\_starrating             & $+3.3437$ & $+0.4081$ \\
prop\_review\_score          & $+5.4841$ & $+0.1088$ \\
prop\_brand\_bool            & $+0.7053$ & $+0.2299$ \\

\textbf{Signs match on every feature.} Both models reward higher star
rating, review score, brand, location, accessibility, and promotion,
and penalise higher displayed and historical price.
\textbf{Magnitudes are not directly comparable.} The two columns use
different scaling conventions (the AI fit z-scores everything on the
$500$-query AI sample, while Problem~1 leaves binaries unscaled), and
the AI magnitudes are MAP estimates with a fixed ridge prior. We
interpret signs and broad relative magnitudes only.

Qualitatively, the AI weights observable quality signals
(review score, location, star rating) much more strongly than humans
do, and is also more price-sensitive in standardised units. This is
consistent with an attribute-driven, rule-like decision process: the
AI reads off the table of features and produces a near-deterministic
choice, while real customers respond to many factors not in the prompt
(photos, brand familiarity, position on the page, trip purpose, etc.).

\paragraph{7c --- Held-out predictive comparison.}
We split the full dataset at the query level using
\texttt{numpy.random.default\_rng(42)}: $10$ in-context examples and
$50$ held-out queries. The held-out prompt (saved by the script to
\texttt{problem7/inputs/ai\_heldout\_prompt.txt}) is sent to ChatGPT
manually; the returned predictions are stored as
\texttt{problem7/inputs/ai\_heldout\_predictions.csv}.

We considered scaling this evaluation to $250$ held-out queries via
five batches of $50$, but the AI's quality degraded under batched
prompting (the agent began producing shortcut answers when asked to
label a long block at once), so we report the cleaner single-prompt
$50$-query evaluation.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Metric.} \emph{Query-level exact-choice accuracy.}
A prediction is counted as correct iff it matches the booked hotel's
\texttt{alt\_id} exactly, or correctly predicts \texttt{NO\_PURCHASE}
on a no-booking query. The MNL hard rule applies the Problem~1
normalised coefficients (continuous z-scored using train-set sample
std, binaries at raw $0$/$1$) and predicts the argmax over hotels and
the outside option (utility $0$). We also report a trivial
\emph{always-\texttt{NO\_PURCHASE}} baseline because $26\%$ of
held-out queries result in no booking, and Wilson $95\%$ confidence
intervals because $n = 50$ is small.

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lrcc}
\toprule
Model & Accuracy & Correct $/$ N & Wilson 95\% CI \\
\midrule
AI agent                       & $0.18$ & $9 / 50$  & $[0.098,\,0.308]$ \\
MNL hard rule                  & $0.26$ & $13 / 50$ & $[0.159,\,0.396]$ \\
Always \texttt{NO\_PURCHASE}   & $0.26$ & $13 / 50$ & $[0.159,\,0.396]$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

The MNL hard rule \emph{ties} the trivial baseline: it predicts
\texttt{NO\_PURCHASE} on every one of the $50$ queries because, under
the correctly-scaled utility computation, no hotel's utility exceeds
the outside option's $u = 0$. The AI agent underperforms the trivial
baseline by 4 correct predictions ($18\%$ vs.\ $26\%$). The
AI--MNL gap of two correct predictions is well within the Wilson CIs
of either model and should not be read as a real difference.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Probability-implied MNL comparison.}
Hard-prediction by argmax discards most of an MNL's information.
We therefore also report the MNL probability-implied summary, where
each hotel contributes its choice probability:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lr}
\toprule
Quantity & Value \\
\midrule
Observed human purchase rate                       & $0.740$ \\
AI agent predicted purchase rate                   & $0.820$ \\
MNL hard-rule predicted purchase rate              & $0.000$ \\
MNL probability-implied purchase rate              & $0.685$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

The probability-implied MNL purchase rate ($0.685$) is the closest
match to the observed $0.740$. The AI's hard prediction
($0.820$) overshoots; the MNL hard rule degenerates to no purchase.
This is the comparison that actually shows signal: the MNL is a
calibrated probability model and should be evaluated through its
distribution, not through its argmax.

\paragraph{Discussion.}
On this dataset, AI agents simulate plausible, directionally reasonable
hotel preferences --- every coefficient sign in the re-estimated MNL on
AI bookings matches the human MNL --- but they are noticeably more
deterministic and more strongly attribute-driven than real customers,
and they are not better predictors of individual booking decisions
than a probability-aware MNL. As exact predictors on $50$ held-out
queries the AI underperforms the trivial \texttt{NO\_PURCHASE}
baseline; even the MNL hard rule only ties that baseline. The MNL
becomes informative once we compare its probability distribution to
observed behaviour, where its implied purchase rate is closest to the
observed rate. Two caveats apply: the prompt-response loop is
performed manually so per-query LLM responses are not checked into
the repo, and the choice of $\lambda = 1$ in 7b is a stabilisation
device rather than a tuned regulariser, which is why we read 7b's
coefficient magnitudes only by sign and broad relative size.
